Why Consonantograms?

  • Egyptian morphemes are phonemically composed only of consonants.
    • Triconsonantal – n f r ‘good, complete’
    • Biconsonantal – p r ‘house’

Why Consonantograms?

  • Egyptian speakers would provide grammatical and lexical modifications to these roots by providing different vowels
    • This is called ablaut

nafir ‘good’ (masc.) vs. nafra-t ‘good’ (feminine + t suffix)

Ablaut in English

  • bear vs. bore vs. burden
  • see vs. saw vs. seen vs. sight

Ablaut in English

  • sing = present tense
  • sang = past tense
  • sung = past participle
  • ____ = noun

Semitic Abjad

“Egyptian for Dummies”

  • But the Semitic mercenaries and delta dwellers who created the Semitic alphabet were not interested in maintaining the old fuddy-duddy Egyptian tradition.

  • Some Semitic speakers must have acquired at least some literacy in Egyptian, and they must have understood the benefits of writing

“Egyptian for Dummies”

  • But it’s clear not all of them knew Egyptian, and they certainly didn’t know the complex logophonographic system that was set in place by the Egyptians, which only a few Egyptians had been able to master

“Egyptian for Dummies”

“Egyptian for Dummies”

  • So, as Amelia Gnanadesikan writes: they created their own bare-bones set of uniconsonantal signs, modeled after the Egyptian ones.
  • This was the “For Dummies” version of writing, stripped of all complexity and redundancy.

From Egyptian to Semitic

Acrophony

  • The value of Semitic symbols, like their Egyptian precursors, were derived through acrophony

Acrophony

  • When the value of an iconic writing character is taken from the first sound of the name of the object it represents

Acrophony in Proto-Canaanite

The symbols and the acrophonic principle were borrowed from Egyptian – the sound values of the graphemes were not!

  • < pr > → < b >, Semitic bēt ‘house’

  • < n > → < m >, Semitic mem ‘water’

  • < ɟ > → < n >, Semitic nahas ‘snake’

  • < r > → < ʕ > , Semitic ʕayin ‘eye’

Acrophony in Proto-Canaanite

Progression of the Semitic Abjad

  • The first Semitic speakers to use the abjad are speakers of “Proto-Canaanite”, beginning around 1700 bce
  • These speakers worked in the Egyptian mines and had relatives living in Middle-Bronze-Age Palestine

Progression of the Semitic Abjad

  • We’ve only got a few inscriptions, all quite short Initially the number of letters within the abjad was 27 – reduced to 22 by the 13th c.
  • The linear orientation was originally anything goes:
    • L → R
    • R → L
    • vertical columns
    • horizontal / vertical boustrophedon

The Alphabet

Gnanadesikan:

“The alphabet has been touted as one of humankind’s greatest inventions – and not without cause. Equally, though, the alphabet is a monument to human stupidity and hidebound conservatism. Originally conceived as a dumbed-down version of writing for the illiterate, it was simple enough to spread easily from one language to the next; however, it was rarely adapted thoughtfully to the new language. Origins of the Greek Alphabet

  • The Semitic abjad was probably brought to Greece by Phoenician traders
  • It’s the first application of the Semitic consonantal alphabet to a non-Semitic language.

Problems

  • The date of borrowing of the Greek alphabet is quite problematic:
    • although the earliest Greek text dates to the 8th c. bce, the earliest form of Greek writing most resembles Phoenician writing from the 11th c. bce
    • Shapes of Letters & Boustrophedon = 11th c. Phoenician

Why the gap?

  • We can always say that the early Greeks wrote on “perishable materials”, but this is an unfalsifiable argument!
  • So scholars keep on digging for those older texts.
